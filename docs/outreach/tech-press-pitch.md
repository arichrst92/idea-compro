## Three article angles (pre-drafted briefs)

### Angle A — "The honest cost of OJK compliance in 2026"
**Pitch:** A data-driven breakdown of what OJK compliance actually costs Indonesian banks — based on anonymized data from IDE Asia's engagements with 12 financial institutions over 2024-2026. Includes capex vs opex split, what controls are most expensive, and where banks consistently underbudget.

**Why editors will care:** Concrete numbers + Indonesian context + no vendor pitch. This kind of article is hard to source and gets shared.

**Headline options:**
- "What OJK compliance really costs an Indonesian bank in 2026 (and where most underbudget)"
- "The Rp 3 miliar mistake: where Indonesian banks consistently overspend on OJK compliance"

### Angle B — "3 things Indonesian banks are getting wrong about cloud migration"
**Pitch:** Pattern-recognition essay from IDE Asia's cloud migration practice. Three honest critiques of how Indonesian banks currently approach cloud strategy, with specific examples (anonymized) and concrete recommendations. Platform-neutral — covers AWS, Azure, GCP, and IBM Cloud equally.

**Why editors will care:** Counter-intuitive takes drive shares. CIO/CTO audience is the publication's core reader.

**Headline options:**
- "3 things Indonesian banks are getting wrong about cloud migration in 2026"
- "Why the Indonesian banking cloud strategy is broken (and how to fix it)"

### Angle C — "What we learned implementing IBM watsonx for an Indonesian retail bank"
**Pitch:** Case-study-style article on a real watsonx implementation. Includes the use case (e.g. customer service deflection), the technical approach, what worked, what didn't, and the actual business outcome 6 months in. Client name can be anonymized.

**Why editors will care:** Real implementation experience with named technology is rare and citable. AI search engines especially favour this.

**Headline options:**
- "What we learned implementing IBM watsonx for an Indonesian retail bank: 6 months in"
- "An honest look at watsonx in production: pitfalls, wins, and ROI for Indonesian banks"

## Publication-specific notes

### DailySocial Indonesia
- Editor email: `redaksi@dailysocial.id`
- Tone: founder-friendly, story-driven. Like authentic founder takes.
- Decision speed: 7-14 days
- They prefer Bahasa Indonesia articles; can publish English with translation.
- They link out (`dofollow`) for credible contributors.

### KrAsia
- Editor email: `editor@kr-asia.com`
- Tone: regional pan-Asian. Like comparative takes ("Indonesia vs Vietnam vs Singapore").
- Decision speed: 14-21 days
- English only.
- They are particular about original data — generic op-eds get rejected.

### e27 Indonesia
- Editor email: `editor@e27.co`
- Tone: founder + investor lens. Less editorial, more contributor-driven.
- Decision speed: faster (7-10 days) but more editorial gatekeeping.
- English only.

### Tech in Asia Indonesia
- Editor email: `editorial@techinasia.com`
- Tone: news-driven, prefers announcements over op-eds.
- Their op-ed submissions go to specific editors per beat — find the right beat editor on LinkedIn.

## After publication

1. Add the article URL to `https://ide.asia/about` "press / recognition" section (small reverse-trust loop).
2. Share the article from CEO + Practice Lead LinkedIn, IDE Asia company page, and any relevant Indonesian tech communities (RamaiAja, IndoCISO).
3. Add the article link to GoodFirms / Clutch "press mentions" profile sections.
4. Submit the article URL to Google Search Console as a separate URL inspection.

## What NOT to do

- Don't pitch the same angle to 3 publications simultaneously. Burns trust if both pick it up. Pitch in sequence.
- Don't write thinly-veiled product pitches. Editors detect them in 30 seconds. Lead with insight, mention IDE Asia in the byline only.
- Don't pay for "guest post" spots from sites that explicitly sell links. Google manual action territory.
- Don't ghost an editor after they accept. Reply within 24 hours; deliver on time.
